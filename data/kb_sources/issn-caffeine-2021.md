## Performance effects

Caffeine is consistently shown to improve exercise performance across a range of
tasks: aerobic endurance, muscular endurance, movement velocity, muscular
strength, sprinting, jumping, and throwing. The effect is well established for
endurance work and increasingly supported for strength and power activities.

Caffeine can also enhance cognitive aspects of performance such as alertness,
vigilance, and reaction time, which matters in sports requiring sustained focus.

## Dosing and timing

Caffeine is most reliably ergogenic at doses of 3 to 6 milligrams per kilogram of
body mass, taken about 60 minutes before exercise. Very high doses (9 mg/kg and
above) do not produce greater benefit and increase the risk of side effects such
as anxiety, jitteriness, and disturbed sleep. Lower doses (under 3 mg/kg) can
still improve performance for some individuals.

Anhydrous caffeine (capsules or powder) produces a more reliable ergogenic effect
than caffeine from coffee, because the dose is precise.

## Individual variation

The performance response to caffeine varies between individuals, influenced by
genetic factors including variation in the CYP1A2 gene that governs caffeine
metabolism and the ADORA2A gene. Habitual caffeine consumers do not necessarily
lose the performance benefit, so withdrawal before competition is not required for
everyone.
