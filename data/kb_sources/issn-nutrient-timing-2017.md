## Carbohydrate timing and glycogen

Carbohydrate availability supports performance in prolonged, high-volume, and
high-intensity training. Athletes performing exhaustive endurance work benefit
from total daily carbohydrate of roughly 8 to 12 grams per kilogram of body weight
to fully replenish muscle glycogen.

When rapid recovery between sessions is required (less than about four hours
apart), consuming carbohydrate at around 1.2 grams per kilogram per hour, ideally
with some protein, accelerates glycogen resynthesis. For athletes training once
per day with ample recovery, the timing of carbohydrate is far less critical than
hitting total daily intake.

## Protein timing

Consuming 20 to 40 grams of high-quality protein, or about 0.25 grams per kilogram
of body weight, every three to four hours best supports muscle protein synthesis
and recovery. Both pre- and post-exercise protein feedings effectively support
training adaptations.

The post-exercise "anabolic window" of opportunity is wider than the once-popular
30-minute claim. Meeting total daily protein needs across well-distributed meals
is more important than precise timing around the workout for most trainees.

## Practical priorities

Total daily energy and macronutrient intake remain the foundation; nutrient timing
is a refinement layered on top of an adequate diet, not a replacement for it.
Pre-sleep protein (30 to 40 grams of casein) can support overnight recovery and
muscle protein synthesis.
