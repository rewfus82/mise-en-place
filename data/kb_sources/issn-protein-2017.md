## Daily protein intake

For building and maintaining muscle mass, an overall daily protein intake of
1.4 to 2.0 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day is sufficient for
most exercising individuals. This is a meaningful increase over the standard
sedentary recommendation. Higher intakes may be warranted in specific situations,
particularly when training volume is high or when the individual is in a caloric
deficit.

During energy restriction (a cutting or fat-loss phase), elevated protein of
roughly 2.3 to 3.1 grams per kilogram of fat-free mass can help preserve lean
body mass while losing fat. Leaner athletes and those undergoing aggressive
deficits sit at the higher end of that range.

## Per-meal dose and distribution

To maximize muscle protein synthesis, aim for a per-meal protein dose of about
0.25 grams per kilogram of body weight, or roughly 20 to 40 grams per serving.
Doses in this range maximize the muscle-building response; substantially larger
single doses do not proportionally increase synthesis.

Distributing protein across the day in three to four meals spaced every three to
four hours supports a sustained anabolic signal, rather than concentrating intake
in one or two large meals. The leucine content of a meal is a key driver of the
response, so complete, leucine-rich protein sources are preferred.

## Timing and source quality

The "anabolic window" after training is wider than once believed. Consuming
protein in the hours surrounding exercise is beneficial, but total daily protein
intake is the dominant factor for adaptation. Both pre- and post-exercise protein
feedings are effective.

Rapidly digested proteins high in essential amino acids and leucine, such as whey,
are most effective at stimulating muscle protein synthesis. Casein consumed before
sleep (roughly 30 to 40 grams) can stimulate overnight muscle protein synthesis
and improve overnight recovery without disturbing fat metabolism.
