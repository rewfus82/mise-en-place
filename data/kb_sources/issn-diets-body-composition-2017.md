## Energy balance is the foundation

A sustained caloric deficit is required to lose fat, and a caloric surplus is
required to gain appreciable muscle, regardless of the specific distribution of
carbohydrate and fat in the diet. No macronutrient ratio overrides the basic
energy-balance requirement for changing body composition.

Many different dietary approaches, from low-carbohydrate to low-fat, can produce
fat loss when they create an energy deficit. Long-term adherence and sustainability
are the strongest predictors of success, so the "best" diet is the one an
individual can follow consistently.

## Protein during fat loss

Higher protein intake protects lean body mass during energy restriction. For
resistance-trained individuals in a caloric deficit, protein in the range of 2.3
to 3.1 grams per kilogram of fat-free mass helps preserve muscle while fat is lost.
Leaner individuals and those dieting aggressively benefit from the upper end of
this range.

## Rate of weight change

A moderate rate of fat loss, on the order of 0.5 to 1 percent of body weight per
week, better preserves lean mass than very rapid weight loss. Aggressive deficits
risk greater muscle loss, reduced performance, and hormonal disruption. When
bulking, a modest surplus limits unnecessary fat gain compared with a large
surplus.
